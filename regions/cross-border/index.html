Eastern and Outlying Records

A small number of records for the surname Зайчик (Zaichyk) appear outside the main Ukrainian, Belarusian, Volhynian, Galician, Kherson, and Crimean documentary clusters. The clearest outlying groups currently include two 1918 Donetsk-region records from Yuzovka and Novo-Komar, along with separate nineteenth-century records from Samara.

Eastern Ukraine Beyond the Main Clusters

The Donetsk-region records differ from the older parish clusters of Right-Bank Ukraine, Left-Bank Ukraine, Volhynia, and Belarus. They belong to a later eastern Ukrainian setting shaped by coal, metallurgy, railways, migration, new towns, and rural settlements connected to the expanding Donbas economy.

The two known entries are both from 1918. One is a birth record for Valentyna Zaichyk in Yuzovka, the industrial settlement that later became Donetsk. The other is a death record for Iliya Zaichyk, who died at one year old and was from Novo-Komar, likely the modern Novyi Komar. Together, they do not yet form a large regional cluster, but they place the surname on the eastern edge of the known documentary distribution.

The contrast between the two records is useful. Yuzovka gives the material an urban-industrial setting: mines, steelworks, migrant labor, workshops, rail connections, and rapid population growth. Valentyna’s record is also urban in social status, identifying her as a mishchanka. Novo-Komar points to a different side of the same broader region: a smaller rural settlement landscape beyond the main industrial town.

These entries come from a period of exceptional disruption. By 1918, late imperial estate language, church and civil record habits, wartime dislocation, revolution, and local economic change overlapped. The Donetsk-region records are therefore best treated as outlying but meaningful traces of families living in a region being transformed by industry, settlement, movement, and political upheaval.

1908 · Yuzovka / Donetsk · birth record

Valentyna Zaichyk, mishchanka

A 1908 birth record from Yuzovka identifies Valentyna Zaichyk in the industrial town world of the Donbas. Yuzovka was the settlement that later became Donetsk, shaped by coal mining, metallurgy, migrant labor, railway connections, and rapid urban growth.

Valentyna’s record identifies the family through the urban-status world of mishchany. That places the entry apart from the rural patterns found in many older regional clusters. Here the surname appears in the social setting of a developing industrial town at the end of the imperial period.

Iliya Zaichyk

A 1918 death record identifies Iliya Zaichyk, who died at one year old, as being from Novo-Komar. Unlike the Yuzovka record, this entry points to a smaller rural settlement environment rather than the main industrial town itself.

Novo-Komar places the surname in the wider Donetsk-region landscape around the industrial and railway world of the Donbas: villages, settlements, religious or civil record systems, household movement, and communities living alongside the region’s new economic geography.

Donbas as an Outlying Record World

These records belong to a region transformed by coal, steel, railways, migration, and nearby rural settlement.

The Donetsk-region records are best treated as outlying attestations rather than as a major family cluster. Their value is contextual: they show the surname appearing in the late imperial and revolutionary-era Donbas, a region shaped by mines, steelworks, railway lines, migrant households, and nearby rural settlements.

Yuzovka and Novo-Komar show two sides of that world. One record belongs to the industrial town that became Donetsk; the other points to a smaller settlement in the wider Donetsk-region landscape. Together, they extend the archive eastward without changing the center of gravity of the surname’s documentary history.

Samara and the Volga Outliers

The Samara records extend the surname beyond the main Ukrainian and Belarusian documentary zones.

The Samara material belongs to a different geographic setting: the Volga region of the Russian Empire. A small number of nineteenth-century parish records from Samara include individuals bearing the Zaichyk surname or closely related transliterated forms.

These records may point toward a wider, still underexplored Russian branch of the surname’s documentary history. Modern surname-distribution patterns under spellings such as Zaychik and Zaichyk likely reflect script, transliteration, migration, and record-access patterns as much as family history.

For now, the Samara entries are treated as outlying attestations. If more Russian church, civil, military, or migration records become available, the Volga and broader Russian material could become a valuable area for future exploration.

Eastern and Outlying Records in the Archive

These entries mark the outer edge of the surname’s known documentary distribution.

The Donetsk-region and Samara records are not currently large enough to stand beside the main clusters of Left-Bank Ukraine, Right-Bank Ukraine, Belarusian lands, Volhynia, Galicia, Kherson, or Crimea. Their value is different. They show the surname appearing in late imperial industrial towns, smaller rural settlements, urban-status records, and a Volga setting.

The Donetsk material is distinctive because it belongs to the rapidly changing world of eastern Ukraine in 1918: Yuzovka’s industrial urban growth and Novo-Komar’s rural settlement context. Samara adds a more distant Volga-region outlier. Together, these records extend the archive beyond the core historical regions while preserving the distinction between major documentary clusters and isolated or emerging attestations.
