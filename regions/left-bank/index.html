Regional cluster

Left-Bank Ukraine

Left-Bank Ukraine is one of the strongest documentary regions for the surname Зайчик (Zaichyk). The records are concentrated in historical Chernihiv and Sivershchyna lands and include early oath-book evidence, Cossack-status households, a Zinkivska Sotnia roll, Orthodox church records, state-peasant entries, and town-status records from the Hetmanate and post-Hetmanate world.

The Hetmanate and the Left-Bank World

The Left-Bank records belong to the Cossack-Hetmanate and post-Hetmanate world of northern and central Ukraine.

Left-Bank Ukraine refers to the Ukrainian lands east of the Dnipro River. In the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries it became the institutional core of the Cossack Hetmanate after the upheavals of the Khmelnytsky era and the division of Ukrainian lands between neighboring powers. The Left Bank preserved a much stronger Cossack administrative structure than the Right Bank, where the Cossack order was suppressed earlier and landlord serfdom returned more fully.

In the Hetmanate, society was organized through a regimental-sotnia system that combined military, administrative, territorial, and judicial functions. Regiments were not simply battlefield units; they were territorial districts headed by colonels and officers, with authority over Cossacks and the civilian population within their jurisdictions. Sotnias functioned as smaller local units within that system, linking households and settlements to a wider administrative order.

This gave the Left-Bank record world a distinct texture. People entered the documentary record not only through parish books, but also through oath books, household rolls, status designations, and local administrative lists shaped by the Hetmanate’s institutions. Even after the gradual absorption of the Hetmanate into the Russian imperial order, many local communities continued to preserve the afterlife of that older world in church and status records.

The Zaichyk records enter this setting directly. The 1654 Prysiazhni knyhy, or oath books, record Trofym Zaichyk as a mishchanyn / townsman in Stol'ne. Later records include multiple Zaichyk households in the Zinkivska Sotnia roll, and Orthodox metrical books from places such as Nosivka and Huzhivka identify Zaichyk families with Cossack status from the late eighteenth into the early nineteenth century. Together, these entries place the surname within one of the most historically distinctive documentary worlds in Ukraine: the regimental landscape of the Hetmanate and its long institutional shadow.

Sivershchyna and the Northern Ukrainian Core

The largest Left-Bank cluster lies in Sivershchyna, the old Chernihiv and Novhorod-Siverskyi region.

Sivershchyna is one of the oldest historical regions of northern Ukraine. It is associated with Chernihiv, Novhorod-Siverskyi, the Desna river world, old princely lands, monasteries, Cossack regiments, and later imperial districts. The region formed part of the northern Left-Bank landscape where older Rusʹ, Lithuanian, Polish-Lithuanian, Cossack, and Russian imperial layers overlapped.

In the Zaichyk record set, Sivershchyna is especially important because it contains the largest identified Left-Bank cluster. Localities such as Masheve, Faiivka, Byryne, Orlivka, and Forostovych place the surname in Orthodox parish books and local status records. These entries include peasants and state peasants, showing the surname in rural communities under the administrative language of the Russian Empire.

Novhorod-Siverskyi gives this cluster its regional center. The surrounding villages and hamlets did not stand apart from the older town world; they belonged to a wider Desna-region landscape of district administration, church institutions, markets, roads, river movement, and historical memory. Reading the village records against Novhorod-Siverskyi helps place the surname within the larger northern Ukrainian setting that organized local life around these smaller communities.

The Sivershchyna material is different from both the Dubno testament evidence and the Kherson civic records. It is less about individual public prominence and more about household continuity across villages, parishes, and social categories. At the same time, it sits in a region with deep Cossack-Hetmanate inheritance, where church record books often preserve the afterlife of older Cossack and town-status distinctions.

Stol'ne and the 1654 Oath Books

One of the earliest known records of the surname appears in the Cossack-era oath books.

1654 · Stol'ne · Prysiazhni knyhy

Trofym Zaichyk, townsman

Trofym Zaichyk is recorded as a mishchanyn / townsman in the 1654 Prysiazhni knyhy. The oath books were produced in the context of the mid-seventeenth-century Cossack state and the oath-taking associated with the Pereiaslav settlement and the regimental order of the Hetmanate.

The Stol'ne entry places the surname in an early civic setting. The word mishchanyn identifies a townsman rather than a rural peasant household, and the source belongs to the political-administrative world of the Cossack-era Left Bank. This makes the 1654 record especially important in the documentary history of the surname.

Regiments, Sotnias, and the Zinkivska Roll

The regimental-sotnia order shaped how territory, households, and service were organized on the Left Bank.

The Hetmanate’s territorial system divided much of Left-Bank Ukraine into regiments and sotnias. A sotnia was not simply a military company. It was also a local administrative unit, with officers, households, obligations, courts, and fiscal responsibilities. Zinkiv belonged to the Hadyatskyi Polk (Hadiatsk Regiment), one of the major regimental territories of the Hetmanate, and the Zinkivska Sotnia formed part of that wider Cossack-administrative landscape.

The 1774 Zinkivska Sotnia roll is therefore a particularly valuable source. It does not record a single baptism, marriage, or burial, but whole households organized within an older territorial order. In the Zaichyk material, the roll preserves multiple surname bearers, including household heads with family members listed beneath them. This places the name inside the administrative and social inheritance of the Hetmanate rather than only in later parish books.

Zinkiv also belonged to a region marked by the unrest of the late eighteenth century. In 1768, during the wider Haidamak uprising associated with the Koliyivshchyna, residents of Zinkiv took an active part in the movement. This was not a folkloric episode. It grew out of a brutal social order of serfdom, landlord power, confessional hostility, and accumulated grievance, and it also involved extreme violence, including anti-Polish and anti-Jewish attacks. That background helps place the Zinkiv records in the harder political and social world of the late Hetmanate era.

Read alongside the Cossack-status entries from places such as Nosivka and Huzhivka, the Zinkiv material broadens the Left-Bank picture. It shows the surname not only among village entries, but within the older regimental geography of central Ukraine: household rolls, Cossack-linked communities, town society, and record systems that still preserved the structures of the Hetmanate even as imperial administration tightened around them.

The Left-Bank material extends across Chernihiv, Sivershchyna, and several additional northern and central Ukrainian localities.

The largest identified cluster appears around Masheve, Faiivka, Byryne, Orlivka, and Forostovych. These records are primarily Orthodox metrical-book entries and include many peasant and state-peasant families.

Konotop provides additional Left-Bank documentation for the surname within the broader northern Ukrainian record pattern.

Parish records preserve formal entries, but they belonged to a wider village world of households, courtship, marriage customs, domestic interiors, and rural community life.

Cossacks, State Peasants, and Townspeople

The Left-Bank material preserves a wider range of status categories than many other regional pages.

The social vocabulary of the Left-Bank records reflects the region’s Cossack and imperial inheritance. Some Zaichyk entries appear as Cossacks, especially in Nosivka and Huzhivka. Others appear as kazenni seliany, or state peasants, especially in Sivershchyna. The 1654 Stol'ne entry records a townsman. Romny supplies another town-status example. Shostka adds a retired-soldier household.

These categories were not decorative labels. They shaped taxation, military obligations, legal jurisdiction, landholding, service, and local community life. A Cossack household was not the same as a private serf household. A state peasant was attached to the state rather than to a private landlord. A townsman belonged to a town estate and civic environment. A retired soldier’s household sat within the imperial military and settlement world.

The Left-Bank record set therefore captures a complex social landscape: Cossack remnants, state peasants, town communities, and imperial military-associated families. This makes the region especially valuable for understanding the surname across multiple social categories.

Eastern Extension: Shostka and Romny

A smaller group of records extends east of the main Chernihiv and Sivershchyna cluster.

Parish record

Shostka

A parish record from Shostka identifies an individual bearing the surname as a retired soldier, along with several children. The entry belongs to an Orthodox metrical-book context but also carries a military-associated status.

Urban status

Romny

A separate record identifies a mishchanyn in Romny, placing the surname in a town context within the eastern portion of the Left-Bank region.

Left-Bank Ukraine in the Archive

The Left-Bank records add early civic evidence, Cossack-era administration, and broad local continuity.

Left-Bank Ukraine is one of the most important documentary regions for the surname because it connects the name to the institutions of the Cossack-Hetmanate world. The 1654 Stol'ne entry places Trofym Zaichyk in the oath-book world of the Cossack era. The Zinkivska Sotnia material places multiple Zaichyk households in a regimental administrative context. Later Orthodox metrical books preserve the surname across Sivershchyna, Chernihiv lands, and additional northern Ukrainian localities.

The social range is also broader than in many regional clusters. The Left-Bank material includes townspeople, Cossack-status households, peasants, state peasants, retired soldiers, and ordinary families. These records show the surname inside the layered world of the Hetmanate and its imperial aftermath: regiments, sotnias, parishes, state categories, town communities, village households, and military-associated families.

The Cossack connection is therefore not incidental. It appears through several documentary pathways: the 1654 oath-book entry, the Zinkivska Sotnia roll, and later church records preserving Cossack status in places such as Nosivka and Huzhivka.
