Volhynia and Galicia

Volhynia and Galicia form the western documentary zone for the surname Зайчик / Zaichyk and the Polish form Zajczyk. The records here belong to an older western Ruthenian world shaped by princely inheritance, town law, Orthodox and Catholic institutions, Polish-Lithuanian rule, Habsburg Galicia, and later imperial administrations.

The Western Ruthenian World

Volhynia and Galicia were old historical regions where princely inheritance, Orthodox institutions, town law, noble estates, and later imperial administrations overlapped.

Volhynia and Galicia belonged to the western Ruthenian world: a region of old Rusʹ principalities, fortified towns, Orthodox churches and monasteries, noble estates, market routes, and mixed legal traditions. In the medieval period, these lands were not peripheral borderlands. They were major centers of Ruthenian political and ecclesiastical life, tied to the history of Galicia-Volhynia, the princely towns of Volodymyr, Halych, Lutsk, Kremenets, and later the wider urban and estate world of early modern Volhynia.

After the medieval period, the region moved through several political orders. Volhynia became part of the Grand Duchy of Lithuania and then the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, while Galicia followed a related but distinct path. After the first partition of Poland in 1772, Galicia entered the Habsburg monarchy as the Kingdom of Galicia and Lodomeria, with Lviv/Lemberg as its capital. These changes did not erase older local society. They layered new administrations, languages, and record systems over older Ruthenian, Orthodox, Catholic, noble, town, and village worlds.

This is why the western records have a different texture from many of the eastern and southern clusters. A Zaichyk or Zajczyk entry from this region might come from a seventeenth-century town book, an Orthodox parish register, a Roman Catholic record, a Polish-language legal environment, a Latin-register church book, a Russian imperial metrical book, or a Habsburg administrative setting. The surname moves across scripts and institutions because the region itself moved through overlapping legal, religious, and political worlds.

The Volhynian material is especially important because it preserves some of the archive’s earliest and richest evidence. The Dubno testaments place the surname inside a seventeenth-century town world of households, property, inheritance, burial obligations, witnesses, and municipal procedure. The Kremenets-area records provide a context of rural life. Galicia and Lviv add a western Catholic and Polish-form layer. Together, these records make the western zone one of the most historically layered parts of the Zaichyk archive.

Volhynia: Towns, Estates, Churches, and Legal Records

Volhynia gives the archive some of its earliest and most socially detailed evidence.

Volhynia was a region of old towns, Orthodox churches, monasteries, noble estates, market roads, villages, and local jurisdictions. Its records were not produced in a single uniform world. A person might enter the written record through a town book, a testament, a church register, an estate context, a court proceeding, or a later imperial metrical book. That variety is part of what makes the Volhynian material so valuable for the surname history.

The strongest early evidence comes from Dubno, where seventeenth-century testament records place Zaichyk families inside an urban legal culture. These were not bare lists of names. They were written acts concerned with houses, orchards, household goods, family obligations, burial instructions, witnesses, and municipal recording procedure. The surname appears there in the lived setting of town households and property-bearing burghers.

This urban Volhynian material differs from the rural clusters found elsewhere in the archive. In many regions, the surname first appears through baptisms, marriages, deaths, godparents, and village affiliation. In Dubno, the early records show people arranging property, naming kin, and entering their affairs into the legal memory of the town. The result is a much fuller glimpse of social position than a normal metrical entry can provide.

At the same time, Volhynia was not only an urban or burgher world. The Kremenets-area material, including Horynka, places the surname in rural Orthodox family life: households, village communities, church books, and the ordinary cycle of birth, marriage, and death. Read together, Dubno and Kremenets show two sides of Volhynia in the archive: the legal and household world of towns, and the world of villages and surrounding estates.

Town Households, Property, and Municipal Procedure

Dubno gives the Volhynian material its strongest early urban setting. In the seventeenth century, the surname appears not only as a name in a church book, but in municipal legal records connected with households, property, inheritance, burial obligations, witnesses, and formal entry into the town book.

The testaments show Zaichyk families acting within the legal world of an early modern town. Houses, orchards, household goods, family relationships, debts, obligations, and burial arrangements all belonged to the practical concerns of town life. These records therefore preserve a social world that is usually invisible in a simple baptism or burial entry.

The people named in the Dubno material appear in a burgher or mishchany environment. That does not mean wealth or prominence in a modern sense, but it does place them inside a recognized urban community with property, church ties, witnesses, and access to municipal procedure. The town book gave those household decisions a durable legal form.

Surmichi, connected with the 1690 testament material, broadens the setting beyond the town center itself. The records point to a wider Dubno-area world of town households, nearby settlements, churches, kinship, and property relations. In this sense, the Dubno testaments are not isolated curiosities; they show how the surname entered the legal memory of Volhynian urban society.

The Dubno Testaments

The testaments preserve family, property, burial, and legal context for early bearers of the surname.

1680 · Dubno

Matvii Zaichyk

The 1680 testament of Matvii Zaichyk places the surname inside the household and legal world of seventeenth-century Dubno. Unlike a brief church record entry, the testament preserves a person arranging his affairs through the municipal record system of the town.

The record connects Matvii to property, family obligations, burial concerns, witnesses, and the formal procedure of entering a final act into the town book. It shows a bearer of the surname not as an isolated name, but as a householder acting within the legal and religious institutions of urban Volhynia.

This makes the Matvii testament one of the most important early records in the archive. It places the surname in a burgher or mishchany setting, where household property, church ties, kinship, and municipal procedure shaped how families appeared in the written record.

1690 · Dubno / Surmichi

Nastasia Stefanova Zaykova and Trokhym Zajczyk

The 1690 testament of Nastasia Stefanova Zaykova, connected with Trokhym Zajczyk of Surmichi, adds another early Volhynian layer to the surname history. It preserves the name in a setting where Ruthenian, Polish-form, town, and local settlement worlds overlapped.

The record is especially valuable because it ties the surname to family relationship, household property, local place, and legal procedure. The connection with Surmichi broadens the setting beyond Dubno itself, placing the family within the wider town-and-village orbit of the Dubno region.

The Polish-form spelling Zajczyk in this material should be read as part of the language and record environment of early modern Volhynia, not as a separate surname. Together with the Matvii record, it shows the surname moving across scripts and institutions inside the same western Ruthenian legal world.

Kremenets, Horynka, and Rural Volhynia

The Kremenets-area material adds rural evidence to the western record zone.

Kremenets gives the Volhynian material a second regional anchor after Dubno. The town stood in one of the old historical landscapes of western Ukraine, associated with hills, monasteries, schools, estates, market life, and the surrounding villages of the Kremenets district. It was not the same kind of setting as Dubno’s seventeenth-century town-book world, but it belonged to the same broader Volhynian region of old towns and rural communities.

Horynka, south of Kremenets, places the surname in the rural environment of Volhynia. Here the record world is not municipal testaments, property acts, and town books, but the ordinary cycle of parish life: baptisms, marriages, burials, godparents, households, village affiliation, and social status labels preserved in metrical books.

This rural material is important because it keeps the western page from being only an urban or legal story. Alongside the Dubno testaments, the Kremenets-area records show the surname in village society: families recorded through church institutions, kinship ties, household continuity, and the religious geography of rural Volhynia.

Read together, Dubno and Horynka show two different ways the surname entered the Volhynian archive. In Dubno, Zaichyk families appear through town law, property, and municipal procedure. Near Kremenets, they appear through Orthodox Church record books and the everyday structure of rural family life.

Galicia and the Habsburg Record World

Galicia adds a related but distinct western layer: Habsburg administration, Lviv/Lemberg, Catholic registers, and Polish-form records.

Galicia followed a different political path from most of Volhynia after the partitions of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth. In 1772, it became part of the Habsburg monarchy as the Kingdom of Galicia and Lodomeria, with Lviv/Lemberg as its capital. This placed the western Ruthenian lands inside a Central European imperial record world, with its own bureaucracy, legal categories, languages, and church institutions.

The region was socially and confessionally complex. Ruthenian/Ukrainian village communities, Greek Catholic parishes, Roman Catholic institutions, Polish urban and noble culture, Jewish communities, Armenian traditions, German-speaking officials and settlers, and Habsburg administrative structures all shaped Galician records. A single surname could move through several of these environments depending on locality, confession, language, and the institution producing the entry.

Lviv/Lemberg gives this material its strongest urban setting. As the capital of Galicia, it was a major administrative, religious, educational, and commercial center of the Habsburg monarchy. Records from Lviv therefore do not belong to the same world as rural Orthodox metrical books or Volhynian town testaments. They come from a large imperial city where Latin-register Catholic records, Polish orthography, Habsburg bureaucracy, and multilingual urban life overlapped.

In this Galician context, the surname appears in Polish form as Zajczyk, including Roman Catholic entries from Lviv. That spelling reflects the language, script, and confessional setting of the record rather than a separate origin for the name. The Galician material therefore broadens the western page: it connects the surname not only to Volhynian Ruthenian and Orthodox worlds, but also to the Habsburg, Catholic, and Polish-form documentary culture of Lviv and Galicia.

Lviv / Lemberg and Polish-Form Records

Lviv places the surname in a major Galician urban center and in Roman Catholic church records.

Lviv, known in the Habsburg period as Lemberg, was the capital of Galicia and one of the major cities of the western Ukrainian and Polish-Lithuanian world. Its records belong to a large urban setting of churches, schools, courts, officials, merchants, craftsmen, religious institutions, and imperial administration.

The Zaichyk material from Lviv appears in Roman Catholic parish records using the Polish form Zajczyk. Examples such as August Gabriel Zajczyk and Ignatius Zajczyk place the surname in a Latin-register and Polish orthographic environment rather than in the Cyrillic Orthodox world found in many other regional clusters.

This spelling difference should be read through the institution that produced the record. In Lviv, a Catholic parish entry could preserve the name in Polish or Latin-script form because that was the language and script of the record environment. It does not require treating Zajczyk as a separate surname from Зайчик; it shows the same name moving through a different western documentary setting.

The Lviv material therefore adds a distinct Galician layer to the archive. It places the surname in the urban Catholic and Habsburg world of Lviv/Lemberg, alongside the older Volhynian evidence from Dubno town books and Kremenets-area church records.

Language, Script, and Confession

The western records preserve the surname across several overlapping documentary worlds.

In Volhynia and Galicia, the surname appears across Cyrillic, Polish, and Latin-register environments. Forms such as Зайчик, Заичикъ, and Zajczyk reflect the language and institution of the source: town books, Orthodox parish records, Roman Catholic registers, Habsburg administrative settings, and Russian imperial metrical books.

This variation belongs to the region’s history. Volhynia and Galicia joined Ruthenian, Polish-Lithuanian, Habsburg, Orthodox, Catholic, urban, rural, and estate-based record systems. The surname’s spellings move through those worlds with the documents that preserved them.

Volhynia and Galicia in the Archive

The western records add age, depth, and social variety to the surname history.

Volhynia gives the archive some of its earliest and most detailed evidence. The Dubno testaments place Zaichyk families in a seventeenth-century town-book world of households, property, inheritance, burial obligations, witnesses, churches, and municipal procedure. The Kremenets-area material adds a rural setting from the same wider Volhynian region.

Galicia adds a different western layer. Lviv/Lemberg records preserve the surname in Polish-form Roman Catholic registers under the Habsburg monarchy, placing Zajczyk in a major Galician urban and Catholic record world.

Together, these records make the western zone one of the most layered parts of the archive: Volhynian town law, rural life, Polish-form Catholic entries, Ruthenian inheritance, Commonwealth institutions, Habsburg Galicia, and later imperial administration.
