Seventeenth-century testaments

Dubno Testaments

Two seventeenth-century testament records from Dubno and Surmichi preserve early documentary appearances of the Zaichyk surname in municipal, legal, family, and property contexts.

Why These Records Matter

The testaments place Zaichyk family members in the urban burgher world of early modern Volhynia.

The Dubno testament records show the surname in formal legal documents from 1680 and 1690. They name Matvii Zaichyk, Trokhym Zaichyk / Zajczyk, and Nastasia Stefanova Zaykova, and they record household property, kinship ties, burial wishes, and municipal procedures. The documents connect the surname to Dubno, Surmichi, burgher life, Orthodox burial practice, and the mixed Ruthenian-Polish written environment of seventeenth-century Volhynia.

1680: Testament of Matvii Zaichyk

Dubno, 5 December 1680

NHAB · fond 694 · inventory 7 · file 816 · fols. 71–71v

Matvii Zaichyk, townsman of Dubno

The testament of Matvii Zaichyk records his final household arrangements and identifies his wife Palahiia, his daughters, his son-in-law Yarema, and the property he wished to distribute. The closing identifies his cross mark and gives the date as 5 December 1680.

Record details

Record type: testament / municipal legal record
Place: Dubno, Volhynia
Date: 5 December 1680
Person named: Matvii Zaichyk
Social context: townsman / burgher household
Associated names: Palahiia, Maryna, Hanna, Nastasia, Kateryna, Yarema, Trokhym Zaichyk

Selected translation

In the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit. Amen. I, Matvii Zaichyk, making my oral will and disposing of my house, my children, and my wife according to my own will, as their own father, arrange as follows.
First, to my wife Palahiia I give, into her authority and lifelong possession, my whole house and all my household property.
To my daughter Nastasia and to my son-in-law Yarema, who lives with me together with my named wife, I entrust the whole household to God and to them.
Having made this my last will, I confirm it myself and sign with a cross by my own hand, since I do not know how to write. + The cross mark of Matvii Zaichyk. Written in the year 1680, December 5.

1690: Testament of Nastasia Stefanova Zaykova

Dubno, 20 March 1690

TsDIAUK · fond 33 · inventory 1 · file 11 · fol. 128v

Nastasia Stefanova Zaykova and Trokhym Zajczyk of Surmichi

The testament of Nastasia Stefanova Zaykova was made in the house of her brother, Trokhym Zajczyk, in Surmichi. The record identifies Nastasia as a widow, states that she had lived in her brother's house for twelve years, and bequeaths an orchard to him in exchange for burial and commemoration obligations.

Record details

Record type: testament / municipal book entry
Place: Dubno and Surmichi, Volhynia
Date: 20 March 1690; entered into the Dubno town books on 22 May 1693
Person named: Nastasia Stefanova Zaykova
Associated name: Trokhym Zajczyk, townsman of Surmichi
Social context: widow, family household, municipal record

Selected translation

The honorable Trokhym Zajczyk, townsman of Surmichi, appeared and requested that the testament of the late Nastasia, widow of Stefan, his sister, be received into the Dubno town books for future memory.
I, the servant of God Nastasia Stefanova, born in Podhorce, formerly under the authority of the nuns, and having lived in the town at Surmichi in the house of my brother named Trokhym Zajczyk for twelve years, make this testament of my last will.
First, I order that my sinful body be buried according to the Christian rite at the church of Saint George in Surmichi.
My paternal orchard, called Napuchowy, I bequeath to Trokhym Zajczyk, my brother, so that he may bury me and do good for my soul.
Given in the house of Pan Trokhym Zajczyk on 20 March 1690. Since she was unable to write, Nastasia Stefanova confirmed this testament with a cross.

What the Two Testaments Show Together

The records preserve names, property, kinship, and local status.

Surname forms

Zaichyk / Zajczyk / Zaykova

The two records preserve related forms of the surname across Ruthenian and Polish written contexts, including Zaichyk, Zajczyk, and the feminine form Zaykova.

Social setting

Burgher and Town Context

The records identify Zaichyk family members in the municipal and burgher world of Dubno and Surmichi, with property, inheritance, witnesses, and town-book procedures.

Religious setting

Orthodox Burial Practice

Both records refer to Christian burial obligations, including burial at the church of Saint George, placing the family in the local Orthodox religious landscape.
